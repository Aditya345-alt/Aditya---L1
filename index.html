Solar Flares & Forecasting

Solar flares are abrupt, intense bursts of radiation caused by the reconfiguration of magnetic fields in the solar atmosphere. The soft X-ray flux can rise by five to six orders of magnitude within minutes, and the strongest M- and X-class events drive severe space-weather disruptions on Earth and in orbit.

10–60 Minute Lead Time
SOVEREIGN Indian Forecasting

Forecasting matters because current operational systems are largely reactive: they often only confirm a flare once the X-ray flux is already rising. Even a 10–60 minute prediction window lets operators safe-mode satellites, reroute polar flights, and warn grid operators.

For ISRO specifically, Aditya-L1 provides India a sovereign, continuous solar X-ray dataset; an indigenous forecasting capability reduces dependence on foreign space-weather feeds and directly supports future missions like Gaganyaan crew radiation safety, navigation constellations, and deep-space communications.

Visible Emission Line Coronagraph (VELC)

Studies the solar corona's dynamics and magnetic fields. It is the primary payload, designed to capture 1,440 images of the Sun daily.

Solar Ultraviolet Imaging Telescope (SUIT)

Images the solar Photosphere and Chromosphere in near Ultraviolet (UV), observing the Sun's dynamic atmospheric layers.

Aditya Solar wind Particle Experiment (ASPEX)

Analyzes solar wind particles (electrons, protons, and alpha particles) and their energy distribution.

Plasma Analyser Package for Aditya (PAPA)

Measures solar wind plasma composition and identifies components contributing to space weather events.

Solar Low Energy X-ray Spectrometer (SoLEXS)

Monitors soft X-ray emissions to study solar flare activities and heating mechanisms of the solar corona.

High Energy L1 Orbiting X-ray Spectrometer (HEL1OS)

Observes hard X-ray emissions from high-energy events in the solar atmosphere, tracking solar flare kinetics.

Magnetometer (MAG)

Measures the magnitude and direction of the interplanetary magnetic field at the Lagrangian Point L1.

A Historic Orbit

Launched on September 2, 2023, onboard the PSLV-C57 rocket, Aditya-L1 completed its 127-day journey to enter its halo orbit on January 6, 2024. Today, it stands as a testament to pioneering space science.
